Do left-handers really die younger?

For decades, a persistent claim suggested that left-handed individuals have a shorter average lifespan. This investigation uses Bayesian analysis to test whether this is a biological reality or a statistical illusion caused by changing cultural norms.

The Core Hypothesis: A Tale of Two Theories

Biological Difference?

This theory suggests an inherent biological or environmental disadvantage for left-handers leads to a shorter life.

This hypothesis argues the "gap" is an illusion. As societal acceptance grew, more people identified as left-handed. Older generations have fewer left-handers, skewing the average age of death in any given year.

The Key Evidence: A Century of Change üìà

The rate of reported left-handedness dramatically increased throughout the 20th century as cultural stigma faded. This is the crucial factor behind the statistical artifact.
